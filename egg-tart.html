Chinese Egg Tarts

Silky, lightly sweet custard nestled in a golden pastry shell — a timeless classic straight from the dim sum cart to your kitchen.

About This Recipe

Chinese egg tarts — or dàn tāt — are one of those recipes that carry so much memory in a single bite. A crumbly, golden pastry shell cradling a perfectly smooth, barely-sweet egg custard that wobbles just so — they've been a staple of Cantonese bakeries and dim sum restaurants for generations, and for very good reason.

This is my simple, home-friendly version that gets you there without a pastry degree. The custard itself is beautifully straightforward: sugar, a touch of condensed milk for a gentle richness, eggs strained for a silky smooth pour, and lukewarm water to bring it all together. That one spoonful of condensed milk is the small secret that makes this custard taste just a little more special than a plain egg-and-sugar version.

The key to perfect egg tarts is patience — gentle heat to set the custard without bubbling, and a proper resting time after baking so everything firms up into that classic smooth, glossy finish. Don't skip the rest. It makes all the difference.

Step-by-Step Instructions

1

Preheat Your Oven

Preheat your oven to 350°C. Set your egg tart moulds on a baking tray and have them ready to fill. Make sure your pastry shells are sitting snugly in the moulds before you pour — you won't want to rush this once the custard is mixed.

2

Dissolve the Sugar

In a large bowl or jug, combine 150g of sugar with 250g of lukewarm water. Stir well until the sugar is completely dissolved — this is important, as undissolved sugar can cause the custard surface to set unevenly. The water should be warm to the touch, not hot, to help the sugar melt easily.

3

Mix in Condensed Milk & Eggs

Stir in 1 heaped tablespoon of condensed milk until fully combined. In a separate bowl, lightly beat 3 eggs, then gently stir them into the sugar mixture. Avoid whisking vigorously — you want as few air bubbles as possible for a smooth, bubble-free custard surface.

4

Strain the Custard

Pour the entire custard mixture through a fine mesh sieve into a clean jug. This removes any egg threads or lumps and is the single most important step for achieving that perfectly silky, smooth custard texture. Pour slowly and don't press — let it flow through on its own.

5

Fill the Tart Shells

Carefully pour the strained custard into each pastry shell, filling to about 80–85% full — the custard will puff very slightly as it bakes. Pour slowly and steadily to avoid creating bubbles on the surface. If any small bubbles appear, pop them gently with a toothpick.

6

Bake & Rest

Carefully slide the tray into the oven and bake for 25 minutes, until the custard is just set with only the faintest wobble in the very centre and the pastry is golden at the edges. Remove from the oven and leave to rest for 10 full minutes before moving or serving — this resting time is what gives the custard its smooth, glossy, perfectly set finish.

💡 Candy's Tips Straining the custard and resting after baking are the two steps you must never skip — they're what separates a great egg tart from a good one. Lukewarm water (not hot) keeps the eggs from scrambling when mixed. Serve warm for that just-out-of-the-bakery experience, or at room temperature — both are wonderful.

🛒 Ingredients

  • Sugar150g
  • Lukewarm water250g
  • Condensed milk1 tbsp
  • Eggs, strained3
  • Egg tart pastry shells8 large

📋 Quick Notes

Storage Best enjoyed fresh and warm. Store leftovers covered in the fridge for up to 2 days. Reheat in a low oven at 150°C for 5–8 minutes to revive the pastry.

Pastry Shells Ready-made egg tart shells are available at most Asian grocery stores and save a lot of time. Homemade shortcrust or flaky pastry shells also work beautifully.

No Bubbles Pour slowly, don't whisk, and pop any surface bubbles with a toothpick before baking for the smoothest finish.
